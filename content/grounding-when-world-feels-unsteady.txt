## The Week Everything Spun

I remember sitting in my apartment, laptop open, and feeling like the room was tilting. Not literally — the walls were fine. But my nervous system had decided we were in free fall. Work stress, a family scare, and too many nights of shallow sleep had stacked up. Someone said "you need to ground yourself" and I almost laughed. Ground myself? I was *in* my body. What did that even mean?

Then I tried one stupidly simple thing: I took my shoes off, went outside, and stood on the grass for ten minutes. Not meditating. Not breathing in a special way. Just standing there, feeling my feet on the earth. By the end of it, the tilt had softened. Not gone — but softer. I’ve leaned on that practice ever since.

> The body is the temple of the soul. Guard it well.
> — attributed to ancient yogic teaching

## What "Grounding" Actually Is

**Grounding** isn’t a metaphor. In energy traditions — from Chinese *qi* and earth-*yin* to yogic *prithvi* (earth element) — the body is understood to exchange something with the ground. You’re not a sealed bag of skin. You’re a system that can get "top-heavy": too much in the head, in worry, in the future or the past. Grounding is the practice of bringing your awareness and your energy back down into your body and, symbolically and sometimes physically, into the earth.

When you’re ungrounded, you might notice racing thoughts, anxiety, spaciness, or feeling like you’re watching your life from the ceiling. When you’re grounded, you feel more *here* — in your feet, in the room, in the moment. It’s not magic. It’s regulation. Your nervous system learns that it’s safe to settle.

::callout icon=🌍 title=Earth Isn’t Just Dirt
Earthing research (connecting bare skin to the earth’s surface) suggests that direct contact with the ground may support circadian rhythm, reduce inflammation markers, and improve mood in some studies. The science is still evolving, but the anecdotal reports from practitioners line up with what traditions have said for a long time: the earth holds a stabilizing frequency.
::end

## Why You Feel Ungrounded

Life does it to all of us. Too much screen time keeps your eyes and mind "up" and forward. Stress and trauma push energy into hypervigilance — your system is scanning for threat instead of resting into safety. Grief, loss, or big change can make the world feel unreal, and you float. Even "good" excitement — a new relationship, a move, a creative high — can leave you buzzy and unanchored.

**The cost** is real: poor sleep, harder decisions, shorter fuse, or that vague sense that you’re not quite in your life. The good news is that grounding is something you can do with your body, in minutes, without any special belief system. You don’t have to believe in chakras or energy to benefit. You just have to be willing to put your feet on the floor and pay attention.

## What You Can Do Today

These are the practices that actually moved the needle for me. No special equipment. No perfect setting.

### Put Your Feet on the Ground

- Stand or sit with bare feet on grass, soil, or (if that’s not possible) on the floor indoors. Concrete or tile still "count."
- Let your weight sink. Feel the soles of your feet, the heels, the balls. Spend at least five minutes there. Ten is better.
- If you’re outside, let your mind rest on the fact that you’re touching the same earth that’s been here for billions of years. You don’t have to feel anything dramatic. Just notice.

### Breathe Down, Not Just In

- On the inhale, imagine the breath going all the way down into your belly and, if it helps, into your legs and feet.
- On the exhale, imagine any tension or "spin" draining down through your body and into the ground. You’re not pushing it away — you’re letting the earth hold it.
- Do this for a handful of breaths whenever you feel yourself floating or frantic.

### Use Your Body’s Weight

- Sit in a chair and feel your sit bones, your back against the seat, your feet flat on the floor.
- Or lie on the floor (yes, the floor). Let the weight of your body be fully supported. Stay for a few minutes. The floor doesn’t need you to do anything. It just holds you.

### One Grounding Object

- Keep something physical that reminds you of "here": a stone from a place you love, a heavy bracelet, a plant you touch when you pass. When you touch it, take one breath and feel your feet. That’s the whole practice.

## The Part Nobody Talks About

**Grounding can bring up stuff.** When you slow down and drop into your body, you might notice tension you’ve been avoiding, or sadness, or anger. That doesn’t mean you’re doing it wrong. It means you’re finally present enough to feel what’s already there. Go slow. If it gets too intense, open your eyes, look around the room, name three things you see. You can come back to grounding when you feel a bit safer.

Some days you’ll do "everything right" and still feel ungrounded. That’s okay. It’s not a test. It’s a relationship — with your body and with the ground. You’re building a habit of return, not a one-time fix.

---

## Coming Back

You don’t have to be spiritual to ground. You don’t have to believe the earth is sentient or that energy is real. You only have to be willing to put your feet on the floor and pay attention. The world will keep spinning. But you can learn, again and again, to land. To feel your weight. To be here.

The ground has been here the whole time. It’s not going anywhere. All you have to do is meet it.
